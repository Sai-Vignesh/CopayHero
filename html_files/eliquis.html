Eliquis FAQs

Medically reviewed by Karen Berger, Pharm.D.
Licensed Pharmacist

How much does Eliquis cost without insurance?

The retail list price of Eliquis without insurance is $780.31 for 60, 5MG Tablet but your free SingleCare savings card can bring down your total to $486.98 for 60, 5mg Tablet. Individuals who do not have a health insurance plan can use our Eliquis coupons to save money and pay a discounted price on this prescription medicine at participating pharmacies. SingleCare discount cards are accepted at many local pharmacies in the United States and Puerto Rico, along with a valid prescription. If you have trouble paying for your prescription, you can also contact the manufacturer for more information about patient assistance programs and other financial assistance options. You may also want to check state-sponsored plans and Medicare’s Extra Help program eligibility. 

How much does Eliquis cost with insurance?

Your pharmacist can calculate your copay with commercial insurance coverage, as costs vary by insurance provider. Or, you can contact your insurance plan for information on your out-of-pocket costs. With a SingleCare savings card, you could pay a reduced price of $486.98 on an Eliquis prescription of 60, 5mg Tablet. This could be cheaper than your insurance company charges. To compare Eliquis prices, ask the pharmacist to run your prescription discount card before buying. Anyone can use a SingleCare card—no eligibility requirements or registration are required.

Does Medicare cover Eliquis and how much does it cost?

Most Medicare Part D and Medicare Advantage plans cover Eliquis, but the out-of-pocket cost varies by plan. At $486.98 per 60, 5mg Tablet, SingleCare may offer a cheaper alternative to your Medicare Advantage or Medicare Part D Plan. Compare costs at a participating pharmacy near you.

Is there a generic of Eliquis?

Eliquis is a brand-name drug that contains the active ingredient apixaban. It is currently manufactured by Bristol Myers Squibb and marketed by Bristol Myers Squibb and Pfizer. Generic Eliquis has been approved by the FDA; however, it is not yet available in the United States. This generic drug may not become available until 2026 due to an extension of the original drug patent. Once generics are available on the market, several manufacturers will likely produce them, which will help the price come down. 

What is Eliquis?

Eliquis was approved by the U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 2012. It can be used for the following conditions.

  • To lower the risk of stroke and other clotting events in people with atrial fibrillation

  • To prevent deep vein thrombosis (DVT, a clot that forms in a deep vein, usually a leg), which may lead to pulmonary embolism (PE, a clot in the lungs) after hip or knee replacement surgery

  • To treat DVT and PE

  • To lower the risk of recurrent DVT and PE 

Eliquis works by blocking a specific blood-clotting protein called Factor Xa to make the blood less likely to clot. Eliquis is available in an oral tablet form, and patients generally take it twice daily. 

What doses of Eliquis are available?

Eliquis is available as an oral tablet in strengths of 2.5 mg and 5 mg. Eliquis is commonly taken by mouth twice daily or every 12 hours, so you will likely receive a 30-day supply of 60 tablets. 

What are the side effects of Eliquis?

Eliquis is a blood thinner, so potential side effects could include a higher risk of bleeding and bruising, such as nosebleeds or bleeding from the gums. 

People who experience any serious side effects of Eliquis should contact their healthcare provider immediately or seek emergency medical help. 

  • Unusual bleeding that lasts a long time

  • Brown, red, or pink urine

  • Red or black stools

  • Coughing up or vomiting blood

  • Unexpected pain or swelling

  • Dizziness

  • Weakness

  • Headaches

Allergic reactions to Eliquis are rare, but you should seek immediate medical attention if you experience signs of an allergic reaction, such as hives, swelling of the face, lips, tongue, or throat, or difficulty breathing or swallowing. 

This is not a complete list of possible side effects. Consult with a healthcare provider about other possible side effects, warnings, and precautions associated with Eliquis. 

Which food should I avoid while taking Eliquis?

Before taking Eliquis, you should discuss possible dietary restrictions with your healthcare provider. They may suggest avoidance of alcohol. Alcohol may have blood-thinning properties that could lead to increased bleeding when combined with Eliquis. 

Eliquis may interact with other medications, such as other blood thinner medications, selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), serotonin norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs), and nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs). Consult your healthcare provider for other possible interactions with Eliquis.

What can I take instead of Eliquis?

Other blood thinners are available that work in a similar way to Eliquis. Other examples of Factor Xa inhibitors include Xarelto (rivaroxaban) and Savaysa (edoxaban). There are currently no generic versions of Xarelto or Savaysa available for purchase. Therefore, brand-name Eliquis alternatives may be just as expensive as Eliquis.

Warfarin is a common alternative to Eliquis since it is widely available as a generic. Warfarin is generally much cheaper than Eliquis. However, the use of warfarin requires more close monitoring. Certain foods with vitamin K, such as kale and other leafy green vegetables, can oppose the actions of warfarin. Dietary restrictions may be needed while taking warfarin. 

If you are considering a change in your medication, consult your healthcare provider, who can answer questions, provide additional information, and offer professional medical advice about your medical condition and treatment. Stopping Eliquis without taking another blood thinner increases the risk of blood clots, stroke, and heart attack, so always follow your healthcare provider’s recommendation.
